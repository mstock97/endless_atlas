Guide to Senso-ji Temple

An In-Depth Guide to visiting the temple and Asakusa Area

Senso-ji temple is an absolute must-visit on any trip to Tokyo. This Buddhist temple is the oldest temple in Tokyo and has been a significant place of worship throughout the city’s history. The temple’s origin is believed to begin in 628, when two fishermen brothers found a statue of Avalokitesvara Bodhisattva (also called Kannon in Japanese), a bodhisattva known for mercy and compassion. The temple continued to grow in both size and prominence over the centuries and became a place of worship for the Shoguns, including those from the Tokugawa Shogunate. As Tokyo (formerly known as Edo) evolved into a significant population and government center by the 19th century, Senso-ji continues to be an important center of worship for people throughout Japanese as well as visitors from around the world.

A Step-by-Step Guide to Visiting

Kaminari-mon Gate

This is the main entrance gate and the traditional starting point for visiting the temple. The gate is flanked by depictions of the gods of wind and thunder, meant to provide protection for the temple. As you pass through the gate, it’s believed that touching the wooden dragon carving on the underside of the red lantern will bring good fortune.

Nakamise-dōri (Nakamise Street)

This busy shopping street leads to the inner temple complex, and is lined with shops selling traditional snacks and goods.

Hozo-mon Gate

This is the inner entrance gate, and marks the threshold of the temple complex itself.

Main Hall courtyard – make an offering and pull your fortune!

Once passing into the courtyard of the Main Hall, there are stalls on either side lining the approach to the Main Hall building that sell amulets, charms, and other traditional temple items. There are also booths where you can pull a fortune from by following the steps below:

  • Deposit into the labeled slot
  • Shake the long metal box until a numbered stick falls out
  • Match the number on the stick to the corresponding drawer (then return the stick to the metal box)
  • Open the drawer and pull the top fortune paper out — this is your fortune!

We were lucky to pull a “Best Fortune”, but Senso-ji has a reputation for a high number of “bad” fortunes, so if you don’t get a good fortune don’t feel too disappointed. There are spots you can tie your bad fortune and leave it behind at the temple, thereby preventing it from “attaching” to you. [picture of fortune tied to wire rack or tree]

Main Hall

The main hall includes both the inner and outer sanctum, and is where visitors pray and conduct other worship practices.
If you would like to participate in purification before entering the main hall, there is both a Jokoro (large central incense burner) and Chōzuya water basin.
