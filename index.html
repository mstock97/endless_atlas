48 Hours in Paris: A Perfect Weekend That Actually Works

Skip the tourist traps. This is the Paris locals know — hidden bistros in the 11th, dawn at Sacré-Cœur, and a two-day itinerary that's genuinely doable.

👩
Sarah Chen
April 15, 2026 · Updated April 20, 2026
⏱ 12 min read 👁 24.8k views

Everyone has an opinion about Paris. Mostly they're wrong — shaped by Instagram, old guidebooks, and movies that haven't reflected the real city in decades. The Paris worth experiencing is noisier, more neighbourhood-obsessed, and significantly more delicious than any postcard suggests.

I've done this trip eleven times. Here's what actually works in 48 hours.

💡 Quick Facts
Currency: Euro (€)  ·  Language: French  ·  Best time to visit: April–June, September–October  ·  Getting there: CDG Airport, ~25 min to centre by RER B

Day 1: The Right Bank Done Properly

Your first morning sets the tone. Do not — under any circumstances — go to the Eiffel Tower before noon. Not because it's bad, but because you'll waste the best two hours of Paris daylight in a queue.

Morning: The Marais

Start in the Marais. Walk from the Place des Vosges — Paris's oldest planned square, arcaded and rose-coloured and usually quiet before 9am — south through to the Jewish quarter on Rue des Rosiers. Get a falafel from L'As du Fallafel (yes, even for breakfast; no, you won't regret it).

The best Paris mornings are ones where you don't have a plan. Have a coffee, watch the boulangeries open, and let the city come to you.

Afternoon: Museum Strategy

You have two choices: the Louvre or the Musée d'Orsay. Not both. Both would take a full day each if you're serious, and half a day each if you're not — which is fine, but exhausting. My preference is d'Orsay for a half-day: it's smaller, the Impressionist collection is extraordinary, and the café on the top floor has a view of the Seine that justifies the price of a coffee.

  • Book tickets online — always, no exceptions, queues are 2+ hours
  • Arrive 15 min before opening for the least crowded experience
  • Visit the less-famous rooms — the Art Nouveau section at d'Orsay is half-empty

Day 1 Evening: Dinner in the 11th

The 11th arrondissement is where Paris actually eats. Oberkampf and Parmentier are lined with the kind of bistros that get written up in food magazines and then quietly stay good for a decade. Book ahead — these places are small.

Day 2: The Left Bank & The View

Start day two early. Set your alarm for 6am, take the Métro to Abbesses, and walk up to Sacré-Cœur. At 6:30am on a weekday, you will share the steps with exactly three other tourists and a handful of locals walking dogs. The view over Paris in the early morning light is the one thing every photo gets wrong — it's better in person, and calmer.

Saint-Germain-des-Prés

The afternoon belongs to the Left Bank. Saint-Germain is tourist-heavy but unavoidable — the café culture here is genuinely historical. Café de Flore and Les Deux Magots are expensive and worth it for exactly one coffee. After that, escape south to the Luxembourg Gardens, which are free, beautiful, and full of Parisians on their lunch breaks.

Evening: The Eiffel Tower (Finally)

Book the summit tickets for 9pm. By then the queues have died down, the sunset is happening, and the city looks exactly like you hoped it would. This is not a cynical move. This is smart sequencing.

Practical Notes

The Paris Métro is excellent and the app is easy to use. Get a Navigo Easy card at any station — tap-and-go for every journey. Don't bother with taxis unless it's very late or you have heavy luggage.

Most restaurants don't open for dinner until 7:30pm at the earliest. Don't show up at 6:30 and look confused. Have a glass of wine at the bar while you wait.

Where to Stay

If budget allows, the 4th or 11th arrondissement puts you in walking distance of most of Day 1. For Day 2, anywhere on the Left Bank works — the 6th is central, the 14th is quieter and cheaper. Avoid chain hotels near CDG unless you're catching an early flight.
